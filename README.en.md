# 9 rules for working with silicon colleagues

I wrote this instruction for myself: how to get value from AI without losing myself or the point of the work.
It might be useful to you too.

[Русская версия](README.md) · on git: https://github.com/kulakov/statement

**The short list:**

1. Draw every conclusion yourself.
2. Send people only what you wrote yourself.
3. Flag the spots in an AI draft where you have doubts.
4. Don't pass AI's work off as your own.
5. Don't cite AI as an authority.
6. Keep your context in git and make it usable.
7. At every moment, keep what you're doing tied to the goal.
8. Research starts with choosing your sources.
9. No quality bar — no value from AI.

Now each rule in detail.

## 1. Draw every conclusion yourself — be ready to walk the path to it out loud

The main rule: you draw every conclusion yourself, with your own head. No conclusion should be made for a person by the machine. The check: be ready to say out loud how you reached the conclusions you present to colleagues. You have to walk the path to a conclusion yourself — and own that conclusion.

Also known as "don't turn the hadi-loop into a hahaha-loop" © Kharitonov.

## 2. Send people only what you wrote yourself

The machine can help you do a great deal. But everything you show other people — write it yourself. Or at least edit it.

That doesn't mean you can't give the team access to documents the machine made. But the thing you want people to actually read — write it yourself. Of course it's fine to give links: here are the materials I drew my conclusions from, they're available to you, read them if you like.

## 3. Flag the spots in AI drafts where you have doubts

If you do rely on a document written by an LLM, treat it as a draft. Read it yourself first and clearly mark every spot where you have doubts; highlight what seems important. Do this before you pass it on. Otherwise it's unclear whether you thought about the text at all, and whether it can be trusted.

Easiest way — right in the text, as a comment:

> Revenue will grow 40% this quarter. // not sure: number came from the AI, haven't checked the primary source

That way the reader immediately sees where you vouch for something and where you don't.

## 4. Don't pass AI's work off as your own

Mark it honestly: this part I did myself, that part was the AI. Admitting the AI did something is fine, nothing to be ashamed of. But lying about it — that kills trust.

## 5. Don't cite AI as an authority

Under no circumstances say "the AI said so" as an argument for a position. We use the machine's borrowed erudition, but citing an LLM's conclusions as an argument is off the table — it's professionally incompetent. "The AI said so" is the best way to plant doubt about your competence and devalue your whole work.

## 6. Keep your context in git and make it usable

Put the project's working context into the team's git — sources, intermediate materials, AI outputs, decisions. For the context to be genuinely usable, you need a minimum of hygiene: search, a convenient interface to the knowledge base, a bot that helps the team work with it.

## 7. At every moment, keep what you're doing tied to the goal

At any moment, keep what you do with AI tied to the goal. Keeping activity tied to the goal is the main thing to care about anyway, even without AI. But in work with the machine it matters especially, because AI often tempts you to widen the scope of the task — and holding focus gets even harder. The human sets the frame: why we're doing this, what exactly needs to be done, by what criterion I'll check quality, and what limits not to cross (which resources to use, what to work with).

## 8. Research starts with choosing your sources

Right after you've formed a hypothesis and set the goal, the task and the criteria, take care to limit the list of sources you'll work with. A research request with no source selection gives an arbitrary result.

## 9. No quality bar — no value from AI

If you can't measure the quality of a result yourself — if there's no way to tell whether what the AI handed you works toward the goal or not — then you get arbitrary quality. And if you get arbitrary quality several times in a row, you're guaranteed to get bad.
